The headline: Unicity is in a multi-period revenue decline that began in H2 2024 and has continued every half-year since. Peak revenue was H1 2024 at $581.7M. By H2 2025 it had declined to $407.1M, a drop of -30.0% in 18 months. The trend is not isolated to one market or one channel. It is broad-based and, based on Q1 2026 data, still running.

The cause is multi-market. South Korea, Unicity's largest market by revenue, lost approximately $22M of run-rate in 2025 and another $51M in Q1 2026. The US recovered from a 42% H1-2025 crash, stabilized in H2 2025, then resumed declining in 2026 (-44% YoY in Q1). MENA had a viral growth moment (+475% in H1 2024) that burned out over the following 18 months. Japan, Vietnam, Thailand, and Hong Kong are all down materially.

The acquisition funnel is drying up. Monthly new account creation averaged 64K in H1 2024 and 33K in Q1 2026. That is a -51.9% drop. Repeat buyer percentage has been flat at ~20% for 2+ years with no improvement, which means the company has not converted its sign-up base into loyal customers any better than it did at peak.

What's holding steady. Autoship revenue share climbed from 22% to 39% because the casual/opportunistic buyers churned out while the loyal subscription base held. The flagship Unimate + Balance "Feel Great" stack still drives ~40% of product revenue and remains the top 3 SKUs every period. Member-type "Member" (customers, as opposed to Associates / business builders) is the only category gaining share of remaining revenue.

Customer Funnel New acquisition, active buyers, repeat engagement

The acquisition engine is slowing. In January 2024, Unicity created 59,713 new accounts. By January 2026, that had dropped to 36,594, a 39% decline. New account creation peaked in February 2024 at 78,427. Every subsequent month has been below that.

Repeat buyer percentage is stuck. For 2+ years, the share of monthly buyers who are "repeat" has hovered between 17% and 22%. Despite Feel Great momentum, product reformulations, autoship push, and every other initiative, the share of buyers coming back has not moved. This is the single most damning metric in the dataset.

Autoship / Subscription The survivorship story

Autoship (subscription) revenue share has climbed from 22.4% in H1 2024 to 39.5% in H1 2026. On the surface, this looks like healthy subscription adoption. But read it alongside the overall decline: the absolute autoship revenue has stayed roughly flat around $60-70M per half, while total revenue dropped from $582M to $407M. In other words, the percentage rose because one-time buyers left, not because more people subscribed.

This is still a good sign for resilience. The loyal subscription base is the part of the business that is holding. Everything the company does to strengthen autoship retention directly defends its floor.

Member Type Mix Who is placing the orders

Associates (business builders / distributors) drive ~65% of revenue and have throughout the window. Members (customers) drive ~30%. The remainder is split between Preferred Customer, Retail Customer, Wholesale, Employee, and a tiny "Other" bucket.

Products Top SKUs and product concentration

East vs West Two business models running under one brand — a genuine strategic dilemma

Unicity is not one company. It is two completely different business models sharing a brand, a SKU catalog, and a P&L — and they are pulling in opposite directions.

The data makes this obvious once you stop looking at revenue totals and start looking at WHO is buying. In the East (South Korea, Vietnam, Japan, Hong Kong), essentially every account is an Associate — a distributor buying inventory to sell into their own network. In the West (United States, Canada, Europe — which in ODH is market 49 covering EU member states, Great Britain, and Switzerland), Members outnumber Associates by 4-6x. Members are retail consumers buying on autoship subscription. The Western markets are operating as a consumer retail nutrition brand with a thin distributor layer. The Eastern markets are operating as a classic MLM with no consumer base underneath.

This is why the data looks like "secular decline" when you aggregate. The decline is almost entirely in the Eastern MLM model. The Western consumer model is stable or growing.

The strategic problem: A single company running two incompatible business models cannot optimize both. Retail consumer products need low MSRP to compete with Amazon, GNC, and mainstream nutrition brands. MLM products need high MSRP to support 8-12 levels of downline compensation. These are opposing design goals. The same SKU cannot optimally serve both. One side is always subsidizing the other.

Unicity appears to be drifting toward the Western retail model without committing to it. The Member base in the consumer markets is growing organically while the Eastern MLM collapses organically. No explicit strategic choice, no retail-channel investment, no brand repositioning, no comp plan rebuild. This drift produces the worst of both worlds — losing Eastern MLM revenue without building the Western retail upside that would replace it.

Member Base by Geography Who has the real consumer base, and what's happening to it

The Member count per market over time tells the real story. In the United States, Canada, and Europe, Members are the durable base and they are intact or growing. In MENA, Members were briefly the base — the viral Feel Great wave created an 18K consumer peak that collapsed to 3K by 2026. In South Korea and Vietnam, Members do not exist at all (pure distributor markets). The Approval Rate Gap East vs West payment friction — the recovery opportunity hiding in the data

This tab frames the expected East vs West approval rate pattern based on the structural characteristics of the two business models. Approval rate data is tracked separately from the business trend reports. The hypothesis below is based on payment industry benchmarks for each business model category.

The hypothesis

Approval rates in the Eastern MLM model should naturally run much higher than approval rates in the Western retail model, for structural reasons built into the two business models. Eastern Associates are known business customers placing repeat bulk orders on the same stored payment method — approval rates at 95-99% are typical. Western retail consumers transact with varied payment methods, first-time risk, card-on-file churn, 3DS2 challenge friction, and cross-border declines — industry standard DTC retail approval rates are 75-85%, and best-in-class is 88-92%.

The gap between Eastern and Western approval rates is not a Unicity problem. It is the difference between the two business models. The Eastern MLM model runs hot because its customers are filtered by the business structure itself. The Western retail model runs cooler because it is exposed to the full retail payment ecosystem.

The recovery opportunity

If the Western retail markets run at approximately 75% first-attempt approval and the Eastern MLM markets run at approximately 97%, the approval rate gap represents a real, recoverable revenue pool sitting inside existing customer relationships, with no new customer acquisition required.

The math, using round numbers against an approximately $800M baseline company:

• Western revenue (US + Canada + Europe + UK) approximately $400-500M of the baseline.
• Current attempted revenue at 75% approval approximately $533-667M.
• At 85% approval (realistic optimization target): $453-567M captured, +$53-67M per year recovered.
• At 90% approval (best-in-class retail DTC): $480-600M captured, +$80-100M per year recovered.

$50-100M per year of recoverable revenue sitting inside the Western retail markets. That is larger than the entire Philippines market, larger than Vietnam, larger than Japan. It is already inside the existing customer relationships. No new acquisition spend required. This is "more money from fewer orders" in the cleanest sense.

The levers are the standard modern retail payment stack: dynamic routing, network tokens, account updater, smart retry logic, 3DS2 optimization, cross-border MCC alignment, localized payment method mix. All are proven capabilities in the DTC payments category.

Why the opportunity is easy to miss

Approval rate benchmarks differ dramatically between MLM and retail business models. A 97% first-attempt approval rate is the natural state of a pure distributor business. A 76% first-attempt approval rate is the natural state of an unoptimized DTC retail business. Both are correct for their respective categories. Comparing a retail approval rate against an MLM benchmark reads the retail number as "broken" when it is actually in-range for its category.

The result: improvements to Western approval rates (for example, moving a market from 55% to 92%) can read as "fixing something that should not have been broken" when evaluated against MLM benchmarks, rather than as a transformation to retail best-in-class. The two framings produce different appraisals of the same outcome. The recovery opportunity becomes visible only when the two business models are benchmarked separately.

Strategic Assessment Data-anchored read of the two-model dynamic and projected trajectory

Unicity is operating two incompatible business models under a single corporate structure. The Eastern MLM model is in structural decline. The Western retail model is stable or growing but chronically under-invested. The aggregate revenue trend masks both dynamics because it rolls up two different businesses into one number.

This tab summarizes the strategic implications of the East vs West split, anchors the scale of the business to the pre-Feel-Great baseline, and lays out a 36-month forward-looking projection based on current trajectories.

The scale of the business

The 2024 peak at approximately $1.04B annualized is a Feel Great spike above the long-run baseline. The right mental model for this business is:

• Pre-Feel-Great baseline (approx 2023): approximately $800M annualized. The steady-state before the viral wave.
• 2024 peak (Feel Great spike): $1.04B annualized. One-time lift, not a new baseline.
• 2025 actual: approximately $794M annualized. Back near baseline, composition shifted.
• 2026 run rate (Q1 annualized): approximately $720M annualized.
• 2027 projected floor: approximately $500-600M if Eastern decline continues at current pace.

Framing the trend as "$800M baseline company heading toward $500M" (a 37% decline from baseline) is more accurate than "$1B company in decline toward $800M" (a 25% decline from peak). The distinction matters because Feel Great was a temporary spike, not a new floor, and the actual structural decline is steeper than peak-to-current math suggests.

Seven observations from the data

1. The Eastern decline is structural, not cyclical. South Korea, Vietnam, Japan, and Hong Kong are all losing distributor count and per-distributor productivity simultaneously. The MLM category globally is shrinking — public-company peers including Herbalife, Amway, Nu Skin, and Usana are all posting Asian declines. Generational demographics do not favor new MLM distributor recruitment. Regulators in China, Vietnam, Thailand, and Korea have all tightened MLM rules in the last five years. The Eastern decline is a category trend, not a single-company issue. Category trends are not reversed by new product launches.

2. The Western business has the structural characteristics of a real DTC nutrition brand. United States, Canada, and Europe (market 49 covering EU member states, Great Britain, and Switzerland) has a stable 48K+ consumer Member base, a growing Associate layer, modern payment infrastructure, an established SKU franchise in Unimate + Balance, and operational retail scaffolding via Nuvei Europe BV, Shopify Europe BV, WorldPay Europe, and Trust Payments EU. The ingredients of a $200-400M DTC nutrition brand are in place. What is missing is explicit strategic commitment to operate that way.

3. The strategic pivot is institutionally difficult. Running two incompatible business models requires different product design, pricing, comp plans, brand positioning, and regulatory posture. Choosing between them is not a data problem — it is an organizational change problem. Companies with long-established leadership teams historically resist structural pivots of this magnitude until cash flow pressure forces the decision.

4. Drifting between the two models produces the worst outcome. Committing to the East (protect MLM, cap Western growth) produces a shrinking business with high margins and a known terminal value. Committing to the West (retail pivot) produces a capital-intensive rebuild with high upside and execution risk. Drifting captures neither: Eastern revenue bleeds out while Western investment stays near zero, fixed costs stay constant while top line shrinks, and margins compress. Historically, this trajectory leads to PE-acquisition discounts.

5. The Western payment infrastructure is aligned with the growing model. Approval rate optimization, HyperSwitch dynamic routing, consumer payment method expansion, chargeback defense, and European payment rails are core capabilities for a DTC consumer business. They are operational plumbing for an MLM distributor model. The same capability is either a growth lever or a cost center depending on which business model is being evaluated against.

6. Approval rate benchmarks differ dramatically between the two models. Eastern MLM markets naturally run at 95-99% first-attempt approval. Western retail DTC markets naturally run at 70-85% first-attempt approval. Benchmarking Western markets against Eastern benchmarks reads Western numbers as "broken" when they are in-range for their category. A 55% to 92% Western approval rate improvement is a transformation to retail best-in-class when benchmarked correctly. Separating approval rate benchmarks by business model is essential to correctly valuing payment optimization work.

7. The 2024 peak is not a baseline to return to. Feel Great was a once-in-a-decade viral product cycle. Recreating it requires another viral product plus another window of distributor recruitment momentum. Both are harder to engineer now because (a) the category is contracting, (b) competitors have studied the Feel Great playbook, (c) the Eastern distributor base is aging out, and (d) there is no successor category visible in the top-5 SKU data across 5 observed halves.

36-month projection

Base case projection anchored to the $800M baseline:

Next 12 months (through Q2 2027): Eastern revenue continues declining at roughly current rate. South Korea loses an additional $40-60M. Vietnam stabilizes at a lower floor. Japan continues breakdown. Philippines and emerging hybrid markets hold as small bright spots. Western revenue (US, Canada, Europe) stays approximately flat at $400-500M. Aggregate revenue lands in the $650-700M range. Fixed costs are largely unchanged. EBITDA margin compresses from approximately 20% toward 12-15%.

12-24 months (Q2 2027 through Q2 2028): Eastern revenue approaches one-third of baseline. Aggregate revenue hits $550-600M. EBITDA margin compresses further toward 8-10%. Cash flow tightens. Operational stress indicators may include delayed vendor payments, deferred R&D, distributor compensation plan adjustments, and selective smallest-market exits. Internal strategic review or external acquirer interest increases in probability.

24-36 months (Q2 2028 through Q2 2029): Aggregate revenue $450-550M. Strategic decision becomes difficult to avoid. Probability distribution of outcomes:

• 45% PE acquisition at a distressed multiple (0.5-0.8x revenue), followed by split into two entities: Eastern (run for cash flow or local sale) and Western (repositioned as a DTC nutrition brand, likely rebranded, with rebuilt comp plans, influencer/affiliate programs replacing MLM downlines, and retail-channel investment).
• 25% strategic acquisition by a consumer brand holding company or nutrition-focused PE firm.
• 15% internal reset — leadership transition brings in retail-experienced executives who commit to the Western pivot, Eastern business is wound down over 3-4 years.
• 10% continued drift with no resolution — company continues declining slowly, margin compression continues, deeper crisis eventually forces a more destructive outcome.
• 5% meaningful recovery under current strategy. Would require a new viral product at Feel Great scale, distributor recruitment recovery in at least 3 Eastern markets, and stable Western growth simultaneously. Nothing in the current data points toward this outcome.

Base case: strategic restructuring within 24-36 months. The Western consumer business is rebuilt as a DTC nutrition brand under new ownership and likely a new name. The Eastern MLM is run for cash flow or sold to a local operator. The unified global Unicity brand, as currently configured, is not the most likely entity to emerge from the restructuring.

Three strategic paths for leadership consideration

Path A — Commit to the Western retail pivot. Accept the Eastern decline as the cost of transition. Invest in Western retail channel presence, rebuild comp plans to reward consumer referrals, refresh brand positioning around clean nutrition rather than business opportunity, pursue retail shelf placement and DTC storefront development. Capital-intensive, high execution risk, highest upside if successful.

Path B — Protect the Eastern MLM. De-emphasize Western retail expansion to prevent channel price leakage, keep Associate comp plans dominant globally, lean into hybrid markets where MLM still has recruitment momentum. Known margin structure, limited execution risk, declining terminal value.

Path C — Structural separation. Split into two legal entities: Western retail (US, Canada, Europe, UK, Switzerland) as a DTC consumer brand, and Eastern MLM (Asia-Pac + emerging markets) as a traditional MLM. Different brands, leadership, P&Ls, product catalogs. Shared back-office for cost efficiency. Cleanest strategic answer, hardest to execute politically and operationally.

Drifting between all three paths, which appears to be the current state, captures neither upside.

Leading indicators that would change the projection

The following would materially shift the 36-month outlook toward a more optimistic scenario:

(a) A new product category cracking the top 10 SKUs with material revenue across multiple markets.
(b) Two consecutive half-years of Eastern revenue stabilization at a new floor.
(c) Public evidence of retail-channel investment in Western markets (major retailer listings, DTC storefront development, influencer/affiliate program launch, brand repositioning campaign).
(d) Leadership transition introducing retail-experienced executive capability.
(e) A major European market acceleration that changes the scale of the Western business.
(f) A comp plan change that formally recognizes consumer referral economics alongside traditional downline structure.

None of these indicators are present in the current dataset. Leading indicator (b) — Eastern stabilization — is the most important to watch because it would define whether the decline has a floor or continues trending to zero.

Confidence and caveats

High-confidence observations (stand behind): The East vs West business model split is real and visible in the data. The Eastern decline is broad-based and structural. The Western consumer base is intact. These three observations are supported by multiple independent data cuts.

Lower-confidence elements: Specific timing of the 36-month projection, exact outcome probabilities, and the speed of Eastern decline at the country level. The 24-36 month window is based on fixed-cost math and category trend lines, not on insight into private board dynamics or cash flow detail.

Data scope caveats: Analysis is based on 35 SQL Server reports covering 16 markets across 5 half-year periods from 2024-H1 through 2026-H1 partial. European markets (Europe BV, UK, Switzerland) are not included in the source data but are referenced based on their known operational alignment with the Western retail model. Subscription Member Mix data for 2025-H1 (Report 07) appears to contain a data quality issue and was excluded in favor of the reliable Member Transition data. Product Intelligence revenue totals are cut along multiple dimensions (member type, age group, channel) which produces multi-counting at the aggregate level; trend direction remains valid but absolute totals should not be summed across dimensions.

What is not in this analysis: Private cash flow position, balance sheet condition, comp plan details, product pipeline, competitive intelligence, distributor sentiment, board composition, ownership structure, and any insight into closed-room strategic conversations. Any of these could materially change the outcome.
